Guadalupe Martinez – California Revealed's Audiovisual Digitization and Preservation Manager

Overview

California Revealed is a statewide digitization initiative which helps public libraries, archives, museums, historical societies, and other heritage groups digitize, preserve, and provide online access to archival materials documenting the state's histories, arts, and cultures. It also provides support and assistance for cataloging, community archiving, and K-12 curriculum development, and provides training and support for Memory Labs at public libraries across California. It began in 2010 as CAVPP as a temporary program funded by the California State Library, and since then has grown in capability, scope, and demand year after year.

As Audiovisual Digitization and Preservation Manager, Guadalupe Martinez manages and oversees California Revealed projects from application review to long-term preservation. This involves coordinating with partner organizations to write basic metadata for and then ship their collections, which are then processed, documented, and given further metadata before being sent to third-party vendors for digitization.

Once digitized, they are checked for fixity and to see if metadata has been assigned correctly, and Guadalupe and other preservation managers communicate with the digitization vendor if discrepancies arise. The initial quality control report is then sent to partners to be signed off, though this is not always done since familiarity with QC varies between organizations.

If requested, partners can receive a physical hard drive containing their digitized materials. Digitized collections are given three points of access: access copies on an AWS web server and two LTO tape preservation copies — one stored in Sacramento and another stored in a vault in Ohio.

Throughout this process Guadalupe works with partners, preservation experts, vendors, and third-party services to resolve issues, build trust, and explain audiovisual preservation practices at multiple levels.

Typical Tasks

As Audiovisual Digitization and Preservation Manager, Guadalupe assesses the feasibility of digitization projects submitted by community heritage partners. She adapts partners’ initial metadata to California Revealed’s standards and coordinates digitization and preservation with external vendors.

Beyond project management, Guadalupe creates educational resources such as handbooks that help demystify digitization processes and the technical language surrounding them. These resources aim to help community partners become better stewards of their materials while strengthening trust between organizations.

A key specialty of the role involves establishing preservation priorities based on FEMA Natural Risk Index scores and Healthy Places Index (HPI) metrics. FEMA data highlights environmental risks such as climate disasters, while HPI evaluates socioeconomic indicators such as education access, employment opportunities, air quality, and water access.

Guadalupe emphasized the importance of prioritizing communities that are already under-resourced in other aspects of life. Gathering this data helps California Revealed understand preservation needs while strengthening justification for continued funding.

Job Market Outlook

Despite major challenges such as the dismantling of the Institute of Museum and Library Services and funding cuts through the National Endowment for the Humanities, the archival job market has continued to grow.

Fee-for-service models, private donations, and community partnerships have helped many archives remain operational. Increased concern for collective memory and community identity has also raised awareness of archival work and the importance of preserving historical materials.

According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, employment for archivists, curators, and museum workers is projected to grow about six percent between 2024 and 2034, slightly faster than the national average. Roughly 4,800 job openings are expected each year due to retirements or career transitions.

Although the field is growing, structural challenges remain. Many archival jobs rely on grant funding, temporary contracts, or small institutional budgets. Community archives and digital preservation programs are expanding, but traditional cultural institutions remain vulnerable to shifts in public funding.

Debates about artificial intelligence also shape perceptions of the profession’s future. Some studies suggest AI may assist with metadata creation, transcription, and document analysis. However, archivists emphasize that archival labor requires interpretation, ethical judgment, and contextual understanding that cannot easily be automated.

WillRobotsTakeMyJob.com estimates archivist positions have roughly a 36% risk of automation, which is considered low-to-moderate risk.

Technical Skill Snapshot

Audiovisual archivists must understand the preservation, restoration, access, and curation of multiple media formats including film, magnetic tape, optical media, and digital files.

Guadalupe’s early work with the Bay Area Video Coalition involved digitizing formats such as open-reel ¼-inch tape, VHS, U-matic, DAT, Maddox formats, and audio recordings.

Handling and digitizing these materials provided a technical foundation that supports her work today coordinating digitization projects and preservation practices for California Revealed partners.

In her current role she also ensures collections are properly rehoused before digitization and helps coordinate transfer logistics between partners and vendors.

Labor Trends

Long-term preservation initiatives often face uncertain funding conditions. California Revealed has historically relied on support from the California State Library, but annual funding structures can create challenges for long-term planning.

Recent federal cuts to the Institute of Museum and Library Services created additional uncertainty. Although funding has since been restored following legal challenges, projects must still meet original timelines and deliverables, compressing work schedules.

Funding restrictions can also limit the autonomy of preservation initiatives. For example, California Revealed relies on cloud infrastructure such as Amazon Web Services and Amazon Simple Storage Service alongside the Archipelago digital asset management system instead of developing a fully bespoke storage system.

To diversify funding opportunities, California Revealed has recently registered as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization. This allows the program to apply for grants and accept private funding sources.
