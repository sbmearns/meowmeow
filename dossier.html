Guadalupe Martinez – California Revealed's Audiovisual Digitization and Preservation Manager

Overview

California Revealed is a statewide digitization initiative which helps public libraries, archives, museums, historical societies, and community heritage organizations digitize, preserve, and provide online access to archival materials documenting California’s histories, arts, and cultures.

The program also provides support for cataloging, community archiving, and K-12 curriculum development while offering training for Memory Labs at public libraries across the state.

Founded in 2010 as the California Audiovisual Preservation Project, the program originally began as a temporary initiative funded by the California State Library but has steadily expanded in scope and demand.

Typical Tasks

As Audiovisual Digitization and Preservation Manager, Guadalupe Martinez manages projects from application review through long-term preservation. This includes coordinating with partner organizations to create metadata and ship collections to digitization vendors.

Once materials are digitized they undergo quality control checks including fixity verification and metadata validation before being delivered back to partner institutions.

Digitized materials are stored in multiple locations including access copies on web servers and preservation copies on LTO tape stored both locally and off-site.

Professional Context

California Revealed is connected to professional archival communities through organizations such as the Association of Moving Image Archivists (AMIA) and the Council on Library and Information Resources (CLIR).

Job Market Outlook

According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, employment for archivists, curators, and museum workers is projected to grow approximately six percent between 2024 and 2034.

Increasing volumes of digital records within government, nonprofit, and corporate sectors are expected to create demand for professionals who can organize and preserve information.

Despite this growth, many archival positions remain dependent on grant funding or temporary contracts, particularly within community archives and smaller cultural heritage institutions.

Technical Skill Snapshot

Audiovisual archivists must understand the preservation, restoration, and access of many media formats including film, magnetic tape, optical media, and digital files.

Guadalupe developed these skills while working with the Bay Area Video Coalition, digitizing formats such as VHS, U-matic, DAT, and open reel tape.

These technical foundations inform the coordination and oversight of digitization projects within California Revealed today.
